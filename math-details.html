1. Introduction

The xT-GK framework extends the traditional Expected Threat (xT) model to properly value goalkeeper distribution decisions. While xT has revolutionized how possession value is quantified in football, it fails to account for the unique context in which goalkeepers operate. This document provides a comprehensive mathematical foundation for xT-GK, detailing the formal definitions, equations, and novel components that make it specifically applicable to goalkeeper analysis.

The mathematical formulations presented here are designed to be rigorous enough for academic peer review while maintaining clear connections to practical football applications. Each component is defined with precise mathematical notation and accompanied by explanations of how it manifests in real-world goalkeeper scenarios.

2. Mathematical Comparison: xT vs. xT-GK

2.1 Traditional xT Framework

The traditional Expected Threat (xT) model, as formulated by Karun Singh, defines the value of possession in a zone \(z\) as the expected goals that will result from having possession in that zone:

\[ xT(z) = \sum_{z'} P(z \rightarrow z') \cdot xT(z') + P(z \rightarrow shot) \cdot xG(z) \]

Where:

  • \(P(z \rightarrow z')\) is the probability of moving the ball from zone \(z\) to zone \(z'\)
  • \(P(z \rightarrow shot)\) is the probability of taking a shot from zone \(z\)
  • \(xG(z)\) is the expected goals value of a shot from zone \(z\)

The value of an action moving the ball from zone \(z\) to zone \(z'\) is then calculated as:

\[ V(z \rightarrow z') = xT(z') - xT(z) \]

2.2 xT-GK Framework

The xT-GK framework extends this model by incorporating goalkeeper-specific factors:

\[ xT\text{-}GK(z, z', p) = xT(z') + PEV(z, z', p) - RAV(z, z', p) \]

Where:

  • \(z\) is the starting zone (typically in the defensive third)
  • \(z'\) is the target zone
  • \(p\) is the pressure level, represented as a multi-dimensional tensor
  • \(PEV(z, z', p)\) is the Pressure Escape Value
  • \(RAV(z, z', p)\) is the Risk-Adjusted Value

2.3 Key Mathematical Differences

2.3.1 Pressure-Adjusted Transition Probabilities

Traditional xT uses fixed transition probabilities, while xT-GK conditions these on pressure:

\[ P(z \rightarrow z') \quad \text{vs.} \quad P(z \rightarrow z' | p) \]

This allows for more accurate modeling of how pressure affects completion probability.

2.3.2 Defensive Zone Revaluation

xT-GK revalues zones in the defensive third using a spatial convolution function:

\[ \phi(z, d) = \alpha \cdot \left(1 - \frac{d}{D_{max}}\right)^{-\beta} \]

Where \(d\) is the distance from the goal, \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\) are parameters controlling the decay rate, and \(D_{max}\) is the maximum distance on the pitch.

2.3.3 Pressure Escape Value

xT-GK introduces a Pressure Escape Value component:

\[ PEV(z, z', p) = \gamma \cdot (p_{level} - p'_{level}) \cdot xT(z') \]

Where \(\gamma\) is a scaling parameter (typically 0.25) and \(p_{level}\) represents pressure intensity.

2.3.4 Risk-Adjusted Value Function

xT-GK explicitly models risk through a Risk-Adjusted Value function:

\[ RAV(z, z', p) = xT(z') \cdot \delta \cdot P_{loss}(z, z', p) \cdot C(z) \]

Where \(\delta\) is a risk aversion parameter, \(P_{loss}\) is the probability of losing possession, and \(C(z)\) is the cost function for zone \(z\).

3. Novel Mathematical Components

3.1 Pressure Tensor Representation

Pressure in xT-GK is represented as a multi-dimensional tensor \(p\) that captures various aspects of the pressing situation:

\[ p = [p_{proximity}, p_{time}, p_{angle}, p_{intensity}] \]

Where:

  • \(p_{proximity}\) is the distance of the nearest opponent (in meters)
  • \(p_{time}\) is the time available for decision-making (in seconds)
  • \(p_{angle}\) is the angle of pressure application (in radians)
  • \(p_{intensity}\) is a measure of pressing intensity (normalized 0-1)

This tensor is then mapped to a scalar pressure level using a function \(f_p\):

\[ p_{level} = f_p(p) = w_1 \cdot p_{proximity}^{-1} + w_2 \cdot p_{time}^{-1} + w_3 \cdot p_{angle} + w_4 \cdot p_{intensity} \]

Where \(w_1, w_2, w_3, w_4\) are weighting parameters that can be calibrated based on empirical data.

3.2 Spatial Convolution

The spatial convolution function \(\phi(z, d)\) revalues zones based on their distance from the goal:

\[ \phi(z, d) = \alpha \cdot \left(1 - \frac{d}{D_{max}}\right)^{-\beta} \]

This function increases the value of zones closer to the goalkeeper's goal, reflecting the higher risk associated with losing possession in these areas.

The adjusted xT value for a zone is then:

\[ xT_{adj}(z) = xT(z) \cdot \phi(z, d) \]

3.3 Temporal Sequence Modeling

xT-GK incorporates temporal discounting to value distribution sequences:

\[ V_{sequence} = \sum_{t=0}^{T} \eta^t \cdot xT\text{-}GK(z_t, z_{t+1}, p_t) \]

Where \(\eta\) is a discount factor (typically 0.8) and \(t\) represents time steps in the sequence.

3.4 Bayesian Inference for Parameter Updating

xT-GK employs Bayesian inference to continuously update model parameters based on observed outcomes:

\[ P(\theta | D) \propto P(D | \theta) \cdot P(\theta) \]

Where \(\theta\) represents model parameters and \(D\) is observed distribution data.

This allows for goalkeeper-specific calibration of the model, adapting to individual tendencies and strengths.

3.5 Non-Linear Value Aggregation

Unlike traditional xT, which uses linear aggregation, xT-GK employs non-linear value aggregation to account for diminishing returns and synergistic effects:

\[ V_{agg}(z_1, z_2, ..., z_n) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} xT\text{-}GK(z_i) + \sum_{i=1}^{n}\sum_{j=i+1}^{n} \lambda_{ij} \cdot xT\text{-}GK(z_i) \cdot xT\text{-}GK(z_j) \]

Where \(\lambda_{ij}\) represents the interaction coefficient between zones \(z_i\) and \(z_j\).

Temporal Discount Factor \(\eta\) 0.7 - 0.9 Controls the value decay over time steps
